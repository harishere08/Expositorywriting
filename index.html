Story Writing

The Writing Process

  1. Pre-writing — Brainstorm ideas; decide on characters, setting, conflict. Use a story map or outline.
  2. Drafting — Write freely. Focus on getting the story down; don't edit yet.
  3. Revising — Improve content: add details, strengthen dialogue, fix pacing.
  4. Editing/Proofreading — Fix grammar, spelling, punctuation.
  5. Publishing/Sharing — Final clean copy.

Elements / Components of a Story

📍 Setting

The where and when of the story. Establishes mood and context.

👤 Characters

The people/beings in the story. Include a protagonist (hero) and often an antagonist (opponent).

⚡ Conflict

The central problem. Types: Person vs. Person, Person vs. Nature, Person vs. Self, Person vs. Society.

📈 Plot

The sequence of events: Exposition → Rising Action → Climax → Falling Action → Resolution.

🎯 Theme

The central message or lesson the story conveys (e.g., "Honesty is the best policy").

🗣️ Point of View

First person (I), Second person (you), or Third person (he/she/they).

In an exam, always establish Setting + Character + Conflict in the opening paragraph. Examiners award marks for a clear story arc — make sure your story has a beginning, middle, and end.
Strong opening: "The night Ali arrived at the empty railway station, he had exactly three rupees and no plan." — This instantly sets place, character, and conflict.

Essay Writing

Developing an Essay Outline

An outline is a roadmap. Always outline before writing. A good outline uses your thesis and topic sentences.

Introduction Hook → Background → Thesis Statement (the "what" and "how" of your argument)
Body Paragraph 1 Topic Sentence → Evidence/Detail → Analysis → Transition
Body Paragraph 2 Topic Sentence → Evidence/Detail → Analysis → Transition
Body Paragraph 3 Topic Sentence → Evidence/Detail → Analysis → Transition
Conclusion Restate thesis → Summarise key points → Fresh insight / call to action

Types of Essays

📖 Narrative Essay

Tells a personal story or experience. Written in first person. Follows a plot-like structure. Uses vivid, sensory description.

Topic: "A time I overcame a challenge." — Narrate the event with dialogue, feelings, and a clear turning point.

📋 Expository Essay

Explains, informs, or describes a topic objectively. No personal opinion. Uses facts, definitions, comparisons, or step-by-step processes. This is the core essay type for this course.

Topic: "How social media affects teenagers." — Present researched facts, causes, and effects without taking sides.

🗣️ Persuasive / Argumentative Essay

Takes a debatable position and uses logic and evidence to convince the reader. Must include:

  • Thesis — a clear, arguable claim
  • Antithesis — acknowledging the opposing view (then refuting it)
  • Evidence — facts, statistics, expert opinion
  • Avoidance of logical fallacies
Thesis: "Term limits improve government by bringing fresh ideas into office."
Antithesis: "Term limits harm government because officials are always inexperienced."
→ Acknowledging the antithesis and then rebutting it strengthens YOUR argument.
Avoid vague words like "wrong," "bad," or "good" in your thesis. Be precise: instead of "Social media is bad," write "Social media significantly reduces adolescent attention spans."

🎨 Descriptive Essay

Paints a picture of a person, place, object, or experience using the five senses. Goal: make the reader feel present. Uses figurative language (similes, metaphors).

"The old library smelled of cedar and old paper; the afternoon light sliced through dusty windows in golden bars."

NarrativeEntertain / share experienceStory structure, first person
ExpositoryExplain / informObjective, fact-based

Reading for Writing II

Drawing Connections

Connecting new ideas to what you already know or to other texts. Three types:

  • Text-to-Self: How does this relate to my own life?
  • Text-to-Text: Does this remind me of another text I've read?
  • Text-to-World: How does this connect to current events or society?

Identifying Themes

A theme is the central message or truth about life expressed in a text. It is NOT the topic — it's what the text says about the topic.

Topic: Friendship  |  Theme: "True friendship endures hardship."

To find the theme: ask "What does this text want me to understand about human life?"

Main Idea and Sub-Ideas

  • Main Idea = the most important point of a paragraph or text (usually stated in the topic sentence or thesis).
  • Sub-ideas / Supporting Details = facts, examples, statistics, anecdotes that develop the main idea.
To find the main idea: cover the examples and ask, "What one claim does this paragraph make?" That's your main idea. Everything else is support.

Inference and Prediction

  • Inference: A conclusion drawn from evidence + prior knowledge. The answer is not directly stated. "Reading between the lines."
  • Prediction: Using clues in the text to anticipate what will happen next.
Text says: "She grabbed her umbrella and sighed at the dark clouds."
Inference: She expects it to rain and is not pleased about it.

Summarising and Synthesising

  • Summarising = condensing the main points of ONE text in your own words.
  • Synthesising = combining ideas from MULTIPLE sources to build a new understanding.
Summarising is like zooming out to see the whole picture; synthesising is weaving multiple pictures together.

Summary Writing

Structure of a Summary

A good summary has three clear parts:

Opening Sentence Name the source/author + state the main idea of the original text.
Body Sentences Cover the key supporting points in order, in your own words. Omit examples and minor details.
Closing Sentence Restate the author's conclusion or overall purpose.

Rules for Writing a Summary (MOM Checklist)

  • M = Main points covered — include only essential ideas
  • O = Organised — follow the original order logically
  • M = My own words — do NOT copy sentences from the original
Never include your own opinion in a summary. A summary reports what the author said, not what you think about it.
Aim for about ⅓ of the original length. If the passage is 300 words, your summary should be ~90–100 words.
Original passage (excerpt): "For over 20 years, biologists have been alarmed that amphibian populations have been declining in both human-populated and seemingly untouched areas. Clear proof has been difficult because there is no reliable data on past population sizes."

Summary: Scientists have been concerned about falling amphibian numbers across various environments for more than two decades, though demonstrating this conclusively remains difficult due to insufficient historical data.

Using Cohesive Devices in Writing

Cohesive devices are words and phrases that connect ideas and create flow between sentences and paragraphs. They guide the reader through your argument.

⏱ Time & Sequence

  • First, second, finally
  • Next, then, after that
  • Meanwhile, subsequently
  • Before, after, during
  • At first, in the end
  • Eventually, previously

➕ Addition & Contrast

  • Addition: furthermore, in addition, also, moreover, besides, not only…but also
  • Contrast: however, on the other hand, nevertheless, although, despite, yet, in contrast, whereas

🎯 Reason, Purpose & Result

  • Reason: because, since, as, due to, owing to
  • Purpose: so that, in order to, to
  • Result: therefore, thus, consequently, as a result, hence, so
Without cohesive devices: "She studied hard. She failed the exam. She was disappointed."

With cohesive devices: "Although she studied hard, she failed the exam. Consequently, she felt deeply disappointed. Nevertheless, she resolved to try again."
In your exam essay, use at least one cohesive device per paragraph — to open it (linking to previous idea) and one to close it (transitioning to the next). Examiners specifically look for this.

Listening Skills

Types of Listening

TypeDescriptionExample
Active Listening Full attention; responding and engaging Nodding, asking follow-up questions in a lecture
Passive Listening Hearing without engaging; not processing deeply Background music while working
Critical Listening Evaluating and analysing what is heard Listening to a debate and assessing arguments
Empathetic Listening Understanding the speaker's feelings and perspective Listening to a friend share a problem
Informational Listening Listening specifically to learn or gather facts Following instructions, attending a lecture
Appreciative Listening Listening for enjoyment or aesthetic pleasure Enjoying a poem or music

Tips for Improving Listening Skills

  • Focus fully — eliminate distractions; make eye contact with the speaker.
  • Don't interrupt — wait for the speaker to finish before responding.
  • Take notes — jot down key words, not full sentences. Handwritten notes improve recall.
  • Ask clarifying questions — "Could you repeat that?" or "What do you mean by…?"
  • Listen for signal words — "Most importantly," "In conclusion," "However" signal key information.
  • Summarise mentally — periodically recap what you've heard to check understanding.
  • Avoid prejudging — don't decide what the speaker means before they finish.
Research shows students who take handwritten notes perform significantly better on recall tests than those who type. Write key words and main ideas, not every word.

Letter Writing

Format — The Seven Parts

#PartDetailsExample
1 Sender's Address Top left. Full address. No name here. 14 Maple Street, Lahore 54000
2 Date One line below address. Full words, no abbreviation. 15 May 2026
3 Recipient's Address Full name, title, department, address. Ms. Ayesha Khan, Director, Admissions Office, UoP
4 Salutation "Dear" + title + surname + comma. Dear Ms. Khan,
5 Subject Line (optional) Bold/underlined. States purpose. Re: Application for the 2026 Scholarship
6 Body Para 1: Purpose | Para 2–3: Evidence | Final: Request See annotated example below
7 Closing & Signature Valediction + 3–4 blank lines + printed name Yours sincerely, [space] Ahmad Raza
Sincerely vs. Faithfully: Use "Yours sincerely" when you know the recipient's name. Use "Yours faithfully" when you don't (opened with "Dear Sir/Madam"). Mixing them is a very common exam error.

Formatting Rules

  • Block format — everything flush left, no paragraph indentation.
  • One blank line between every section.
  • Font: 11–12pt professional (Times New Roman, Calibri).
  • Margins: 1 inch (2.5 cm) on all sides.
  • Single-spaced within paragraphs.
  • Date written in full words — never "15/5/26".

Tone & Language Techniques

Situation✗ Avoid (Informal)✓ Prefer (Formal)
ContractionsI can't attendI am unable to attend
ColloquialismsI wanted to touch baseI am writing regarding…
Emotional languageI am furious about thisI respectfully contest this decision
Vague requestsGet back to me when you canI would appreciate a response by 30 May 2026
Unknown recipientHi there / To Whom It May ConcernDear Sir/Madam,
Passive voiceThe report was reviewed by meI reviewed the report

Three Core Principles

  • Be precise — name specific dates, amounts, and references. Vagueness undermines authority.
  • Stay objective — express grievances through documented facts, not emotional reaction.
  • One idea per paragraph — each paragraph has a single function: state, support, or request.
State your purpose in the very first sentence of the body. Don't bury it: "I am writing to apply for the 2026 Merit Scholarship" — not "I have been a student at…" (backstory first). Examiners want your purpose upfront.

Vocabulary Skills

Inclusive Terms — Definition

Inclusive language is language that is respectful, unbiased, and avoids discrimination based on gender, age, ability, race, or social background. It ensures communication is fair and representative of all groups.

Key Principles of Inclusive Language

  • Gender-Neutral Terms — Instead of "chairman" → "chairperson"; use "they/them" for unknown gender.
  • Avoid Ableist Language — Instead of "handicapped" → "person with a disability."
  • Respect Identity & Pronouns — Use a person's preferred name and pronouns.
  • Be Culturally Aware — Instead of "blacklist/whitelist" → "deny list/allow list."
  • Eliminate Ageist Language — Instead of "elderly" → "older adult."
Exclusive: "Hey guys, let's get started on this project."
Inclusive: "Hey everyone, let's get started on this project."
→ "Guys" may unintentionally exclude non-male participants. "Everyone" ensures inclusivity.

Denotation and Connotation

Denotation is the dictionary definition — the literal, exact meaning of a word.

Connotation is the emotional or cultural meaning a word carries beyond its definition — its associations and feelings.

WordDenotation (Literal)Connotation (Feeling)
HomeA building where someone livesWarmth, safety, belonging (+)
SnakeA reptile without legsDeceit, danger, treachery (−)
ChildlikeResembling a childInnocent, curious, playful (+)
ChildishResembling a childImmature, silly, irresponsible (−)
SlimThin in bodyElegant, healthy, attractive (+)
SkinnyThin in bodyUnhealthily thin, sickly (−)
CheapLow in pricePoor quality, miserly (−)
AffordableLow in priceAccessible, good value (+)
Writers choose words for their connotations, not just their denotations. Politicians say "slim government" not "skinny government." Advertisers say "pre-owned" not "used." Spotting this in a reading passage = inference skill.
Same denotation, different connotation:
"She was assertive in the meeting." (positive — confident, strong)
"She was aggressive in the meeting." (negative — hostile, threatening)
Both describe similar behaviour — the word choice reveals the writer's attitude.

⚡ Last-Minute Quick Reference

  • Essay structure: Intro (hook + thesis) → Body (1 idea/paragraph) → Conclusion (restate + fresh insight)
  • Persuasive thesis: Must be debatable. Test it: can someone argue the opposite? ✓ = good thesis
  • Summary rule: MOM — Main points · Organised · My own words. No opinions. ~⅓ original length.
  • Paraphrase ≠ copying: Change sentence structure AND vocabulary, not just synonyms. Always cite.
  • Letter closing: Know name → "Yours sincerely" | Don't know name → "Yours faithfully"
  • Connotation: Childlike (+) vs Childish (−) | Slim (+) vs Skinny (−) | Affordable (+) vs Cheap (−)
  • Cohesive devices: Time (first/then/finally) · Contrast (however/although) · Result (therefore/consequently)
  • Inclusive language: Person-first, gender-neutral, culturally sensitive. "Everyone" not "guys."
  • Story elements: Setting, Character, Conflict, Plot (SCCP), Theme, Point of View
  • Types of listening: Active, Passive, Critical, Empathetic, Informational, Appreciative
